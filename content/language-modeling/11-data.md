# 11 — Data: Building a Pretraining Corpus

The uncomfortable truth of the field is that data quality moves the loss curve more than almost
any architectural cleverness. Two models with the same architecture and compute but different data
are not close. Put flatly: data is the single most significant competitive advantage in model
training, and it is the part labs guard most closely precisely because it is where the advantage
lives. This chapter is about where pretraining data comes from and the pipeline that turns raw web
scrape into something worth training on — end to end, from Common Crawl WARC files to a filtered,
deduplicated, decontaminated corpus you can tokenize and train on. It is unglamorous and it is where
a lot of the real advantage lives.

## Where the data comes from

The bulk of pretraining data is web text. The canonical raw source is **Common Crawl**, a
years-running public scrape of the web, distributed as enormous archives in two forms: **WARC**
(the raw HTTP responses, including full HTML) and **WET** (a crude pre-extracted plain-text
version). It is petabytes, it is mostly garbage, and turning it into training data is the pipeline
below. You should generally extract from WARC yourself rather than trust the WET files, because WET
extraction is low quality — the difference in the final model is large.

Beyond raw web text, real corpora mix in higher-quality curated sources: books (PG-19 and others),
code (GitHub), academic papers (arXiv), reference (Wikipedia), and increasingly synthetic data
generated by other models. The mixture weights across these sources are themselves a tuned
hyperparameter that meaningfully affects the model. It is worth knowing the lineage of the public
web corpora that pushed the state of the art, because each one is essentially a published version of
this pipeline with different choices: **C4** (heuristic-filtered Common Crawl), **The Pile**
(curated multi-source), **RefinedWeb** (aggressive filtering + dedup, showed web-only can beat
curated mixes), **Dolma** and **FineWeb** (large, open, carefully documented pipelines), and
**DCLM** and **Nemotron-CC** (model-based quality filtering, the current frontier of open data).

## Three stages, three quality bars

Training data organizes into three phases, and the point is that the quality bar rises as you
go:

1. **Pre-training** — the largest volume, the most permissive quality bar. This is where filtered
   web text dominates; you need trillions of tokens and cannot afford to be too picky.
2. **Mid-training** — a smaller volume of higher-quality, often domain-specific data (more code,
   more math, curated reference), frequently used to extend context length or shore up specific
   capabilities.
3. **Post-training** — instruction-following data, preference data, and synthetic data for
   alignment, the smallest volume and the highest quality bar per token.

The same raw source can feed different stages at different filtering thresholds. This staging is why
"data mixing" is not a single decision but a schedule.

## The pipeline, stage by stage

Raw Common Crawl to trainable tokens is a sequence of filters, each removing a large fraction of
what enters it. Get any stage wrong and you either keep garbage or throw away good text. You
implement each of these stages as a function operating on a `Document`, wiring them into one pass
over the WARC records read with a library like `warcio`.

### 1. Text extraction

HTML is not text. You have to pull the actual content out of the markup, boilerplate, navigation,
ads, and scripts. The WET files do a crude job; better pipelines re-extract from the raw WARC HTML
with tools tuned to find the main content and drop the chrome. A good pipeline converts HTML to
clean text/markdown (using extractors such as `resiliparse` or `trafilatura`, or a markdown
conversion step), with pre- and post-processing to fix encoding and strip residual formatting. This
stage matters more than it sounds: bad extraction leaves menus and cookie banners interleaved with
content, which teaches the model junk. Your VLM work on document layout is the same problem in a
different medium: separating content from boilerplate.

### 2. Language identification

Run a language classifier — a **fastText** language-ID model is the standard choice — score each
document, and keep the languages you want above a confidence threshold. Most English-centric models
filter to English (and lose multilingual ability as a result). If you care about Urdu or code, you
keep those explicitly, and you accept that your tokenizer and data mix now have to represent them,
which costs capacity. fastText is the recurring tool here because it is fast enough to run over
petabytes and accurate enough for this coarse decision.

### 3. Quality filtering

The big one, and the stage where the field has moved the most. You want text that looks like the
kind of writing you want the model to learn from, and you want to drop spam, gibberish,
keyword-stuffed SEO pages, and machine-generated sludge. There is a clear progression from rules
to models:

- **Heuristic filters:** rules on document statistics. Fraction of lines ending in punctuation,
  ratio of symbols to words, mean word length, fraction of stop words present, fraction of
  duplicated lines, minimum/maximum document length, boilerplate patterns. The **Gopher** quality
  rules and the **C4** rule set are the canonical examples, and a Gopher-style filter is the
  standard starting point. Cheap, interpretable, catch the obvious garbage.
- **Classifier filters:** train a lightweight classifier (again typically **fastText**) to
  distinguish "high quality" reference text from random web text, and keep documents it scores
  highly. You build exactly this: positive examples drawn from curated high-quality sources
  (Wikipedia-referenced pages, OpenWebText-style links, or a known-good corpus), negative examples
  drawn from raw Common Crawl, then keep documents the classifier scores above a threshold. The key
  empirical point is that model-based filtering **substantially outperforms** heuristics (this is
  the lesson behind DCLM and Nemotron-CC), at the cost of more compute. The risk is that
  the classifier bakes in the biases of whatever you called "high quality," so it can quietly narrow
  the distribution.

You also run a **harmful-content / toxicity filter** at this stage (fastText classifiers for NSFW
and toxic content) to drop the worst categories before they ever reach the
model. Filtering is destructive and you cannot fully undo it, so it is worth iterating: inspect
samples of what you are keeping and what you are dropping, because filters have surprising failure
modes (a too-aggressive symbol filter can delete all your code and math).

### 4. Deduplication

The web is enormously redundant: the same article, license text, and template appears thousands of
times. Duplicated data hurts in two ways. It wastes training compute re-learning the same thing,
and heavy duplication drives memorization and degrades loss-per-unique-token. Dedup is one of the
highest-return stages — aggressive deduplication improves models at fixed compute across multiple
papers — and you implement it at two granularities.

**Exact dedup** is easy: hash each document (or each line, for line-level dedup that strips repeated
boilerplate) and drop repeats. You implement exact line deduplication by counting line hashes across
the corpus and removing lines that appear too many times.

**Fuzzy (near-duplicate) dedup** needs the MinHash + LSH machinery, and this is the piece with real
math worth understanding. The goal is to find document pairs with high **Jaccard similarity** —
the size of the intersection over the union of their n-gram sets — without doing the O(n²) all-pairs
comparison over billions of documents. The construction:

- Represent each document as its set of word **n-grams** (use n-grams, not single words, so word
  order matters).
- **MinHash:** apply many independent hash functions to the n-gram set; for each hash keep the
  minimum value. The key theorem is that the probability two documents share a given MinHash value
  equals their Jaccard similarity. So a signature of `k` MinHashes estimates Jaccard as the fraction
  of matching entries — you have compressed each document to a fixed-length signature whose
  agreement is an unbiased estimate of similarity.
- **LSH (banding):** split the `k`-entry signature into `b` bands of `r` rows each (`k = b·r`). Two
  documents become **candidate** duplicates if they match exactly in at least one band. The
  probability that a pair with true Jaccard `s` becomes a candidate is `1 − (1 − s^r)^b`. That
  formula is an S-curve in `s`: you choose `b` and `r` to place its steep threshold right where you
  want the cutoff (e.g. tune so that pairs above ~0.8 Jaccard are almost always caught and pairs
  below are almost always skipped). Only candidate pairs are then compared exactly, collapsing the
  quadratic blowup into something tractable.

```text
P(candidate | jaccard = s) = 1 - (1 - s^r)^b
```

Increasing `r` sharpens and raises the threshold (fewer false positives, more misses); increasing
`b` lowers it (catch more, more false positives). This single S-curve is the entire tuning story of
fuzzy dedup.

### 5. PII removal

Scraped web text contains personal information — emails, phone numbers, IP addresses, physical
addresses, identifiers. Responsible pipelines detect and **mask** it: you write maskers that replace
emails, phone numbers, and IP addresses with placeholder tokens (returning both the scrubbed text
and a count of replacements). This is partly a legal and ethical obligation and
partly a quality issue: you do not want the model emitting someone's phone number. The alignment
stage handles some behavior, but removing it from pretraining is more reliable than
trying to train it away later.

### 6. Decontamination

The last filter, and the direct link back to evaluation. Before training, remove from the corpus any
documents that overlap with your evaluation benchmarks (n-gram overlap against the benchmark items),
so your later eval numbers measure capability rather than memorized test answers. Decontamination is
a data-pipeline responsibility even though the consequence shows up in evaluation.

## The data mixture and curriculum

After you have clean data from multiple sources, you decide how much of each to include. More code
improves reasoning and structured output even for non-code tasks. More math helps quantitative
reasoning. Too much of any narrow source narrows the model. The mixture ties back to the
three-stage view: many runs use a **curriculum**, changing the mixture over training — front-loading
broad filtered web data in pre-training, then increasing the fraction of high-quality curated,
math, and code data toward mid-training, sometimes with a final **annealing** phase on a small
very-high-quality set. The mixture and schedule are tuned like any other hyperparameter, on smaller
runs, using the scaling-law transfer idea covered earlier. Domain-specific sources also get
domain-specific handling: GitHub code needs **license filtering** (permissive licenses only) and its
own dedup; academic papers get their own quality classifiers.

## Synthetic data

Increasingly, part of the corpus is generated by existing models: rephrasings of web text into
cleaner form, generated question-answer pairs, generated reasoning traces, generated code with
tests. This dominates the post-training stage and is creeping into mid-training. It is powerful (you
can manufacture data in exactly the distribution you want) and dangerous (you can amplify the
generating model's errors and biases, and over-reliance risks a narrowing feedback loop). For your
domain this is directly relevant: your synthetic conveyor-belt detection pipeline and synthetic
document generation are the vision analog, and the same principles apply. Control the distribution
deliberately, validate against real held-out data, and do not let synthetic data become a closed
loop that drifts away from reality.

## The legal layer

Data is not legally free. Most content on the internet is copyrighted by default,
so building a corpus means reasoning about fair use, licensing, and terms of service — GitHub code
licenses are the sharpest example, but the question applies to books, news, and scraped web text
generally. This is not a footnote: it constrains what a production system can legitimately train on,
and it is a live area of litigation. Treat provenance and license as metadata you track through the
whole pipeline, not an afterthought.

## Why this is where the leverage is

Architecture converged. Almost everyone uses the pre-norm RMSNorm RoPE SwiGLU GQA decoder covered
earlier. The optimizer is Adam-family. The scaling laws are known. What separates a strong model
from a weak one at the same size and compute is increasingly the data: the extraction quality, the
filtering (especially the shift to model-based classifiers), the dedup, the mixture, the synthetic
augmentation. This is both good news and bad news for a practitioner. Good, because you can improve
a model enormously without inventing new architecture, just by improving data. Bad, because it is
slow, iterative, and hard to measure, with long feedback loops. It is also the least publishable and
therefore the most under-documented part of the field, which is exactly why building the whole
pipeline yourself is worthwhile and why the labs with the best data guard it.

## Key takeaways

Data quality moves the loss curve more than architecture at this point, so the pretraining data
pipeline is where much of the real advantage lives — it is the single biggest competitive
edge. The pipeline turns raw Common Crawl (extract from WARC, not the crude WET) into trainable
tokens through extraction (resiliparse/trafilatura), language ID (fastText), quality filtering
(Gopher/C4 heuristics plus, increasingly, fastText classifiers trained high-quality-vs-web that
outperform rules), deduplication, PII masking, and decontamination — each stage dropping a large
fraction. Deduplication and model-based quality filtering are the highest-return stages;
deduplication uses MinHash to estimate Jaccard similarity and LSH banding to avoid all-pairs
comparison, with the S-curve `1 − (1 − s^r)^b` as its entire tuning knob. Training is staged
(pre-, mid-, post-training) with a rising quality bar, and the mixture across web, code, math, and
curated sources is a tuned hyperparameter often changed on a curriculum with a final high-quality
anneal. Synthetic data lets you manufacture the distribution you want but risks amplifying errors
and closing feedback loops, so validate it against real data. Copyright and licensing are real
constraints, not footnotes. The same principles govern your synthetic vision-data pipelines.
